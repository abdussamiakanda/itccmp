The brief history of domain wall

Posted: 1 October 2021
Written by: Md Abdus Sami Akanda

Introduction

Magnetic domains are typically drawn as a line. In reality it has width.

Magnetic domain theory was developed by French physicist Pierre-Ernest Weiss, who, in 1906, suggested existence of magnetic domains in ferromagnets. He suggested that large number of atomic magnetic moments (typically $10^{12}-10^{18}$) were aligned parallel. The direction of alignment varies from domain to domain in a more or less random manner, although certain crystallographic axis may be preferred by the magnetic moments, called easy axes.

Definition: A domain wall can be generically described as topological solitons which occur whenever a discrete symmetry is spontaneously broken.

A discrete symmetry is a symmetry that describes non-continuous changes in a system.

A topological soliton or "toron" occurs when two adjoining structures or spaces are in some way "out of phase" with each other in ways that make a seamless transition between them impossible. One of the simplest and most commonplace examples of a topological soliton occurs in old-fashioned coiled telephone handset cords, which are usually coiled clockwise. Years of picking up the handset can end up coiling parts of the cord in the opposite counterclockwise direction, and when this happens there will be a distinctive larger loop that separates the two directions of coiling. This odd looking transition loop, which is neither clockwise nor counterclockwise, is an excellent example of a topological soliton.

Magnetic origin

In magnetism, a domain wall is an interface separating magnetic domains. It is a transition between different magnetic moments and usually undergoes an angular displacement of 90° or 180°. A domain wall is a gradual reorientation of individual moments across a finite distance. The domain wall thickness depends on the anisotropy of the material, but on average spans across around 100-150 atoms.

The energy of a domain wall is simply the difference between the magnetic moments before and after the domain wall was created. This value is usually expressed as energy per unit wall area.

The width of the domain wall varies due to the two opposing energies that create it: the magnetocrystalline anisotropy energy and the exchange energy ($J_\mathrm{ex}$), both of which tend to be as low as possible so as to be in a more favorable energetic state. The anisotropy energy is lowest when the individual magnetic moments are aligned with the crystal lattice axes thus reducing the width of the domain wall. Conversely, the exchange energy is reduced when the magnetic moments are aligned parallel to each other and thus makes the wall thicker, due to the repulsion between them (where anti-parallel alignment would bring them closer, working to reduce the wall thickness). In the end an equilibrium is reached between the two and the domain wall's width is set as such.

An ideal domain wall would be fully independent of position, but the structures are not ideal and so get stuck on inclusion sites within the medium, also known as crystallographic defects. These include missing or different (foreign) atoms, oxides, insulators and even stresses within the crystal. This prevents the formation of domain walls and also inhibits their propagation through the medium. Thus a greater applied magnetic field is required to overcome these sites.

Note that the magnetic domain walls are exact solutions to classical nonlinear equations of magnets (Landau-Lifshitz model, nonlinear Schrödinger equation and so on).

Types of walls

There are many types of domain walls, but here we'll only talk about two: Bloch wall and Néel wall.

Bloch wall

A Bloch wall is a narrow transition region at the boundary between magnetic domains, where magnetic moments rotate out of $xy$ plane. It is named after the physicist Felix Bloch.

Bloch domain walls appear in bulk materials, i.e. when sizes of magnetic material are considerably larger than domain wall width [Ref]. In this case energy of the demagnetization field does not impact the micromagnetic structure of wall. The mixed cases are possible as well when demagnetization field changes the magnetic domains (magnetization direction in domains) but not the domain walls [Ref].

Néel wall

A Neel wall is a narrow transition region at the boundary between magnetic domains, where magnetic moments rotate in the $xy$ plane. It is named after the French physicist Louis Néel.

It consists of a core with fast varying rotation (where the magnetization points nearly orthogonal to the two domains) and two tails where the rotation logarithmically decays. Néel walls are the common magnetic domain wall type in very thin films where the exchange length is very large compared to the thickness. Néel walls would spread across the whole volume if not for magnetic anisotropy.

Magnetocrystalline anisotropy

A ferromagnetic material is said to have magnetocrystalline anisotropy if it takes more energy to magnetize it in certain directions than in others. These directions are usually related to the principal axes of its crystal lattice. It is a special case of magnetic anisotropy.

Causes

The spin-orbit interaction is the primary source of magnetocrystalline anisotropy. It is basically the orbital motion of the electrons which couples with crystal electric field (a static electric field produced by a surrounding charge distribution) giving rise to the first order contribution to magnetocrystalline anisotropy. The second order arises due to the mutual interaction of the magnetic dipoles. This effect is weak compared to the exchange interaction and is difficult to compute from first principles, although some successful computations have been made.

Practical relevance

Magnetocrystalline anisotropy has a great influence on industrial uses of ferromagnetic materials. Materials with high magnetic anisotropy usually have high coercivity, that is, they are hard to demagnetize. These are called "hard" ferromagnetic materials and are used to make permanent magnets. For example, the high anisotropy of rare-earth metals is mainly responsible for the strength of rare-earth magnets. During manufacture of magnets, a powerful magnetic field aligns the microcrystalline grains of the metal such that their "easy" axes of magnetization all point in the same direction, freezing a strong magnetic field into the material.

On the other hand, materials with low magnetic anisotropy usually have low coercivity, their magnetization is easy to change. These are called "soft" ferromagnets and are used to make magnetic cores for transformers and inductors. The small energy required to turn the direction of magnetization minimizes core losses, energy dissipated in the transformer core when the alternating current changes direction.

Thermodynamic theory

The magnetocrystalline anisotropy energy is generally represented as an expansion in powers of the direction cosines of the magnetization. The magnetization vector can be written $\mathbf{M} = M_s(\alpha, \beta, \gamma)$, where $M_s$ is the saturation magnetization. Because of time reversal symmetry, only even powers of the cosines are allowed. The nonzero terms in the expansion depend on the crystal system (e.g., cubic or hexagonal).

The general form of anisotropy energy can be written as $$\mathcal{E}_{an} = \sum_i f(\mathbf{m}_i)$$

Uniaxial anisotropy

More than one kind of crystal system has a single axis of high symmetry (threefold, fourfold or sixfold). The anisotropy of such crystals is called uniaxial anisotropy.

In the case of uniaxial model, i.e., if the easy axis is aligned with a specific axis $z$, the anisotropy energy can be written as, $$ \begin{align*} & f(m) = K_o + K_1 m_z^2 + K_2 m_z^4 + K_3 m_z^6 + \cdots \\ \Rightarrow & f(m) = K_o + K_1 \cos^2 \theta + K_2 \cos^4 \theta + K_3 \cos^6 \theta + \cdots \end{align*} $$ where, $\theta$ is the angle between easy axis and magnetization direction.

For the simplest case, only the quadratic term is significant, $$f (m) = - K_1 (\mathbf{m} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}})^2$$ where, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ represents the anisotropy direction. The parameter $K_1$, often represented as $K_u$, has units of energy density and depends on composition and temperature.

When $K_1 > 0$, the axis is an easy axis and for $K_1 < 0$, the axis is an hard axis. The shape anisotropy originates from the dipole-dipole interaction and hence depends on the geometrical shape of the magnet.

If the $z$ axis is taken to be the main symmetry axis of the crystal, the lowest order term in the energy is $$\frac{E}{V} = K_1 \left(\alpha ^2+\beta ^2\right) = K_1 \left(1-\gamma^2\right)$$ The ratio $E/V$ is an energy density (energy per unit volume). This can also be represented in spherical polar coordinates with $\alpha = \cos \phi \sin \theta, \beta = \sin \phi \sin \theta,$ and $\gamma = \cos \theta$ : $$ \frac{E}{V} = K_1 \sin^2\theta$$
Figure: Uniaxial anisotropy energy plotted for 2D case. The magnetization direction is constrained to vary on a circle and the energy takes different values with the minima indicated by the vectors in red.

The minima in this energy with respect to $\theta$ satisfy $$\frac{\partial E}{\partial \theta} = 0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial \theta^2} > 0$$ If $K_1 > 0$, the directions of lowest energy are the $±z$ directions. The $z$ axis is called the easy axis. If $K_1 < 0$, there is an easy plane perpendicular to the symmetry axis (the basal plane of the crystal).

The Motion of 180° domain wall in uniform dc magnetic field

We start with the Landau-Lifshitz-Gilbert equation, $$\frac{d\mathbf{m}}{dt} = - \gamma \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_\text{eff} + \alpha \mathbf{m} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{m}}{\partial t}$$ where $\mathbf{m} = \frac{\mathbf{M}}{M_o}$.

$M_o$ is the saturated magnetization, which is assumed to be a constant with time and space, and varies only with temperature.

There are a few energies associated to the effective field, which is defined by $$\mathbf{H}_\text{eff} = - \frac{\partial E}{\mu_o \partial \mathbf{M}}$$ We consider an infinite medium, whose magnetization at any point is $\mathbf{M}$, where $|\mathbf{M}| = M_o$, a constant. The medium is to have a uniaxial crystalline anisotropy energy. The symmetry axis, which is an easy axis, is taken to be the $z$ axis of a Cartesian system.

The energy density associated with the anisotropy is: $$-\frac{K M_z^2}{M_o^2}$$ The exchange energy density is written in the form: $$\frac{A}{M_o^2}\left( |\nabla M_x|^2 + |\nabla M_y|^2 + |\nabla M_z|^2 \right)$$ It will be assumed that $\mathbf{M}$ is a function of $x$ alone, so that this exchange term takes the form: $$\frac{A}{M_o^2} \left| \frac{\partial \mathbf{M}}{\partial x} \right|^2$$ and the demagnetizing field is $$4\pi M_x$$ There is thus a magnetostatic energy density, $$2\pi M_x^2$$ In the presence of a uniform dc magnetic field $H_o$ applied along the $z$ axis, a Zeeman energy density, $$-H_oM_z$$ The total energy per unit area (in the $yz$ plane), $\mathcal{E}$, is then given by $$\mathcal{E} = \int_{- \infty}^{+ \infty} \left( -H_oM_z + 2\pi M_x^2 -\frac{K M_z^2}{M_o^2} + \frac{A}{M_o^2} \left|\frac{\partial \mathbf{M}}{\partial x}\right|^2 \right) dx$$ If $\mathbf{M}$ is referred to a polar coordinate system with the $z$ axis as pole and $\phi$ measured from the axis: $$M_x = M_o \sin\theta \cos\phi \text{ ;} \\ M_y = M_o \sin\theta \sin\phi \text{ ;} \\ M_z = M_o \cos\theta$$ And since $M_o$ is constant with respect to $x$, the magnetization element in spherical coordinate is, $$d\mathbf{M} = M_o d\theta \hat{\theta} + M_o \sin \theta d\phi \hat{\phi}$$ So we get, $$ \begin{align*} & \left| \frac{\partial \mathbf{M}}{\partial x} \right|^2 = \left( \frac{\partial M_x}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial M_y}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial M_z}{\partial x} \right)^2 \\ \Rightarrow & \left| \frac{\partial \mathbf{M}}{\partial x} \right|^2 = M_o^2 \left[ \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \sin^2\theta \left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\right)^2 \right] \end{align*} $$ Then, the energy $\mathcal{E}$ takes the form: $$E = \int_{- \infty}^{+ \infty} \left[ -H_o M_o \cos\theta + 2\pi M_o^2 \sin^2\theta \cos^2\phi -K \cos^2\theta + A \left\{\left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \sin^2\theta \left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\right)^2\right\}^2 \right] dx$$

Effective field in spherical coordinate

Now the effective field is, $$H_\text{eff} = \frac{2A}{\mu_o M_o} \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{m}}{\partial x^2} - \frac{1}{\mu_o M_o} \frac{\partial f(m)}{\partial m} + H_d +H_o$$ where, the first term is exchange field, the second term is anisotropy field, the third term is demagnetization field and the last term is the external magnetic field. Now, we'll construct the effective field in the spherical coordinate form. In the spherical coordinate, $${\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf {\hat {x}} \\ \mathbf {\hat {y}} \\ \mathbf {\hat {z}} \end{bmatrix}} = {\begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta \cos \phi & \cos \theta \cos \phi & -\sin \phi \\ \sin \theta \sin \phi & \cos \theta \sin \phi & \cos \phi \\ \cos \theta & - \sin \theta & 0 \end{bmatrix}} {\begin{bmatrix} {\boldsymbol {\hat {r}}} \\ {\boldsymbol {\hat {\theta }}} \\ {\boldsymbol {\hat {\phi }}} \end{bmatrix}} $$ $$\hat{x} = \sin \theta \cos \phi \hat{r} + \cos \theta \cos \phi \hat{\theta} - \sin \phi \hat{\phi}$$ $$\hat{y} = \sin \theta \sin \phi \hat{r} + \cos \theta \sin \phi \hat{\theta} + \cos \phi \hat{\phi}$$ $$\hat{z} = \cos \theta \hat{r} - \sin \theta \hat{\theta}$$ The form, $$ \begin{align*} & \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{m}}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial^2 (m_x)}{\partial x^2}\hat{x} + \frac{\partial^2 (m_y)}{\partial x^2}\hat{y} + \frac{\partial^2 (m_z)}{\partial x^2}\hat{z} \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{m}}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial^2 (\sin\theta \cos\phi)}{\partial x^2} (\cos \theta \cos \phi \hat{\theta} - \sin \phi \hat{\phi}) + \frac{\partial^2 (\sin\theta \sin\phi)}{\partial x^2} (\cos \theta \sin \phi \hat{\theta} + \cos \phi \hat{\phi}) + \frac{\partial^2 (\cos\theta)}{\partial x^2} (- \sin \theta \hat{\theta}) \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{m}}{\partial x^2} = \left( \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right) \hat{\theta} + \left( 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right) \hat{\phi} \end{align*} $$ The first term, $$ \begin{align*} & \frac{\partial^2 (\sin\theta \cos\phi)}{\partial x^2} (\cos \theta \cos \phi \hat{\theta} - \sin \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( \cos \theta \cos \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} - \sin \theta \sin \phi \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right) (\cos \theta \cos \phi \hat{\theta} - \sin \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \left[ - \sin \theta \cos \phi \left( \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 - \cos \theta \sin \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \cos \theta \cos \phi \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \left[ - \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi \left( \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 - 2 \cos^2 \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \cos^2 \theta \cos^2 \phi \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 - \sin \theta \cos \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right] \hat{\theta} + \left[ \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \left( \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 + 2 \cos \theta \sin^2 \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} - \cos \theta sin \phi \cos \phi \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} + \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \sin \theta \sin^2 \phi \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right] \hat{\phi} \end{align*} $$ The second term, $$ \begin{align*} & \frac{\partial^2 (\sin\theta \sin\phi)}{\partial x^2} (\cos \theta \sin \phi \hat{\theta} + \cos \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left[ \cos \theta \sin \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \cos \phi \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right] (\cos \theta \sin \phi \hat{\theta} + \cos \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \left[ - \sin \theta \sin \phi \left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \sin \theta \cos \phi \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right] (\cos \theta \sin \phi \hat{\theta} + \cos \phi \hat{\phi}) \\ & = \left[ - \sin \theta \cos \theta \sin^2 \phi \left( \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 + 2 \cos^2 \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \cos^2 \theta \sin^2 \phi \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \left(\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \sin \theta \cos^2 \phi \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right] \hat{\phi} \end{align*} $$ The third term, $$ \begin{align*} & \frac{\partial^2 (\cos\theta)}{\partial x^2} (- \sin \theta \hat{\theta}) \\ & = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 \right] \hat{\theta} + \left[ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right] \hat{\phi} $$ Now, the anisotropy energy density is, $$f_{an} = - K_1 m_z^2 = \left. - K_1 \frac{\mathbf{M}}{M_o} \cdot \frac{\mathbf{M}}{M_o} \right|_{\hat{z}} $$ The anisotropy field along $z$ axis is, $$ \left. - \frac{1}{\mu_o} \frac{d f_{an}}{d \mathbf{M}} \right|_{\hat{z}} = \left. \frac{2 K_1}{\mu_o} \frac{\mathbf{M}}{M_o^2} \right|_{\hat{z}} = \frac{2 K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \cos \theta \hat{z} $$ $$ {\begin{bmatrix} H_{an_r} \\ H_{an_\theta} \\ H_{an_\phi} \end{bmatrix}} = {\begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta \cos \phi & \sin \theta \sin \phi & \cos \theta \\ \cos \theta \cos \phi & \cos \theta \sin \phi & -\sin \theta \\ - \sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \end{bmatrix}} {\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \frac{2 K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}} $$ $$ H_{an_\theta} = - \frac{2 K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta $$ $$ H_{an_\phi} = 0 $$ The demagnetization field along the $x$ axis is, $$H_d = - 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \cos \phi \hat{x}$$ $${\begin{bmatrix} H_{d_r} \\ H_{d_\theta} \\ H_{d_\phi} \end{bmatrix}} = {\begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta \cos \phi & \sin \theta \sin \phi & \cos \theta \\ \cos \theta \cos \phi & \cos \theta \sin \phi & -\sin \theta \\ - \sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \end{bmatrix}} {\begin{bmatrix} - 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \cos \phi \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}} $$ $$H_{d_\theta} = - 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi$$ $$H_{d_\phi} = 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi$$ The external magnetic field along the $z$ axis is, $$H_{ex} = H_o \hat{z}$$ $$ {\begin{bmatrix} H_{{ex}_r} \\ H_{{ex}_\theta} \\ H_{{ex}_\phi} \end{bmatrix}} = {\begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta \cos \phi & \sin \theta \sin \phi & \cos \theta \\ \cos \theta \cos \phi & \cos \theta \sin \phi & -\sin \theta \\ - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 \right\} - \frac{2 K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta - 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \cos \theta cos^2 \phi - H_o \sin \theta \right] \hat{\theta} + \left[ \frac{2 A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right\} + 4 \pi M_o \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \right] \hat{\phi} \\ \Rightarrow & H_{eff} = H_{eff_\theta} \hat{\theta} + H_{eff_\phi} \hat{\phi} \end{align*} $$

The equations of motion

So, now let's get back to our dynamical equation of motion. We will derive the LLG equation in the spherical coordinates. $$\frac{\partial \mathbf{m}}{\partial t} - \alpha \mathbf{m} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{m}}{\partial t} = - \gamma \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_\text{eff}$$ Let, $$d\mathbf{m} = d\theta \hat{\theta} + \sin \theta d \phi \hat{\phi}$$ $$\frac{\partial \mathbf{m}}{\partial t} = \dot{\theta} \hat{\theta} + \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \hat{\phi}$$ And, $$\mathbf{m} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{m}}{\partial t} = {\begin{bmatrix} 0 & \hat{\theta} & \hat{\phi} \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \dot{\theta} & \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix} } = - \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \hat{\theta} + \dot{\theta} \hat{\phi} $$ Last and, $$- \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{H}_\text{eff} = - {\begin{bmatrix} 0 & \hat{\theta} & \hat{\phi} \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \mathbf{H}_{eff_\theta} & \mathbf{H}_{eff_\phi} \end{bmatrix} } = \mathbf{H}_{eff_\phi} \hat{\theta} - \mathbf{H}_{eff_\theta} \hat{\phi} $$ Okay, this is the last and, $${\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \dot{\theta} \\ \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \end{bmatrix}} - \alpha {\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ - \sin \theta \dot{\phi} \\ \dot{\theta} \end{bmatrix}} = \gamma {\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \mathbf{H}_{eff_\phi} \\ - \mathbf{H}_{eff_\theta} \end{bmatrix}} $$ The equations of motion are then, $$\dot{\theta} + \alpha \sin \theta \dot{\phi} = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right\} + 4 \pi \gamma M_o \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi $$ and, $$ \sin \theta \dot{\phi} - \alpha \dot{\theta} = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 \right\} - \frac{2 \gamma K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta - 4 \pi \gamma M_o \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi - \gamma H_o \sin \theta $$

Static Domain wall

For a static domain wall (without any external field i.e., $H_o = 0$) and for transverse domain wall, $\phi = \pm \pi/2 $ or for constant $\phi (z,t) = \phi_o$, the equation of motion: $$ \begin{align*} & \dot{\theta} + \alpha \sin \theta \times 0 = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \times 0 + \sin \theta \times 0 \right\} + 4 \pi \gamma M_o \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \\ \Rightarrow & \dot{\theta} = 0 \end{align*} $$ and, $$ \begin{align*} & \sin \theta \times 0 - \alpha \times 0 = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( 0 \right)^2 \right\} - \frac{2 \gamma K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta - 4 \pi \gamma M_o \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right) - \gamma \times 0 \times \sin \theta \\ \Rightarrow & 0 = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \frac{2 \gamma K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} = \frac{2 \gamma K_1}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta \\ \Rightarrow & A \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} = K_1 \sin \theta \cos \theta \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} = \frac{K_1}{A} \sin \theta \cos \theta \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \partial \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right) = \frac{1}{\Delta^2} \sin \theta \cos \theta \partial \theta \\ \Rightarrow & \partial \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 = 2\frac{1}{\Delta^2} \sin \theta \cos \theta \partial \theta \end{align*} $$ Integrating, $$ \begin{align*} \Rightarrow & \left(\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \right)^2 = \frac{1}{\Delta^2} \sin^2 \theta + c \end{align*} $$ For a given, $c = 0$, $$ \begin{align*} \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{\Delta} \sin \theta \\ \Rightarrow & \frac{\partial \theta}{\sin \theta} = \frac{1}{\Delta} \partial x \\ \Rightarrow & ln \left(\tan \frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \frac{x}{\Delta} \\ \Rightarrow & \theta = 2 \tan^{-1} e^{\frac{x}{\Delta}} \end{align*} $$ where, $\Delta = \sqrt{A/K_1}$ is the domain wall width and $x$ gives the position of the domain wall.

You maybe skeptic about how $\Delta$ can be width, so, I will show you the dimension of it. $$\left[\sqrt{\frac{A}{K_1}} \right] = \left[ \sqrt{\frac{\mathrm{J/m}}{\mathrm{J/m^3}}} \right] = \left[ \sqrt{\mathrm{m^2}} \right] = \left[ \mathrm{m} \right] $$ Now, I'll show some numeric solution using the equation that I just derived. Here, you can see that, when $x = 0$ the angle of the magnetic moments is $90^o$. And, at $x = 214 \mathrm{\; nm}$, the angle is $180^o$. The domain wall is situated at the origin.

import math

A = 13*10**(-12)
K = 3.75*10**5
x = 0

delta = math.sqrt(A/K)

theta = 2*math.atan(math.exp(x/delta))

print("Width = " + str(delta))
print("Angle = " + str(theta*180/math.pi))
Width = 5.887840577551898e-09
Angle = 90.0

import math

A = 13*10**(-12)
K = 3.75*10**5
x = 214*10**(-9)

delta = math.sqrt(A/K)

theta = 2*math.atan(math.exp(x/delta))

print("Angle = " + str(theta*180/math.pi))
Angle = 180.0

From here, we can come to the conclusion that the domain wall is the direct consequence of the solution of the LLG equation. You can check that, I didn't assume anything regarding domain wall.

LLG equation in spherical coordinates in terms of easy and hard axis

Anisotropy field: $$H_{an} = - \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \phi \hat{x} + \frac{2 K_\parallel}{\mu_o M_o} \cos \theta \hat{z}$$ $${\begin{bmatrix} H_{an_r} \\ H_{an_\theta} \\ H_{an_\phi} \end{bmatrix}} = {\begin{bmatrix} \sin \theta \cos \phi & \sin \theta \sin \phi & \cos \theta \\ \cos \theta \cos \phi & \cos \theta \sin \phi & -\sin \theta \\ - \sin \phi & \cos \phi & 0 \end{bmatrix}} {\begin{bmatrix} - \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \phi \\ 0 \\ \frac{2 K_\parallel}{\mu_o M_o} \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}} $$ $$ H_{an_\theta} = - \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi - \frac{2 K_\parallel}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta $$ $$ H_{an_\phi} = \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi $$ The effective field in the spherical coordinate, $$H_{eff} = \left[ \frac{2 A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 \right\} - \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi - \frac{2 K_\parallel}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta - H_o \sin \theta \right] \hat{\theta} + \left[ \frac{2 A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right\} + \frac{2 K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi \right] \hat{\phi}$$ $$H_{eff} = H_{eff_\theta} \hat{\theta} + H_{eff_\phi} \hat{\phi} $$ The equations of motion are, $$\dot{\theta} + \alpha \sin \theta \dot{\phi} = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ 2 \cos \theta \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \sin \theta \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} \right\} + \frac{2 \gamma K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \sin \phi \cos \phi$$ and, $$ \sin \theta \dot{\phi} - \alpha \dot{\theta} = \frac{2 \gamma A}{\mu_o M_o} \left\{ \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} - \sin \theta \cos \theta \left( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \right)^2 \right\} - \frac{2 \gamma K_\perp}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta \cos^2 \phi - \frac{2 \gamma K_\parallel}{\mu_o M_o} \sin \theta \cos \theta - \gamma H_o \sin \theta $$
