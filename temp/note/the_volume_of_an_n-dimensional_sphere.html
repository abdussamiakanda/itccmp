The volume of an n-dimensional sphere

Posted: 31 December 2020
Written by: Md Abdus Sami Akanda

The volume of an n-dimensional sphere (n-sphere) enclosed by a (n-1) dimensional surface

This is a very interesting problem in mathematics. And now, we'll solve this problem from zero.

We'll use the same trick as to find the area under a Gaussian function, $$y = e^{-x^2}$$ Here is a plotting of this function using python. import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 100)
y = np.exp(-x**2)

plt.plot(x, y)
plt.xlabel("x")
plt.ylabel("y")
plt.show()
Now, the area below this curve is simply defined by the integral, $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} dx$$
This integral is a tricky one. But, it turns out that the square of this integral can be evaluated much easily. $$ \begin{align*} & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} dx\right)^2 \\ = & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_1^2} dx_1\right) \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_2^2} dx_2\right) \\ = & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2)} dx_1 dx_2 \end{align*} $$
It is much easy, because we can write it in polar coordinates. The transformation equations are, $$x_1 = r \sin \theta \\ x_2 = r \cos \theta \\ x_1^2 + x_2^2 = r^2$$ $$dx_1 dx_2 = rdrd\theta$$ Now, the integral becomes, $$ \begin{align*} & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2)} dx_1 dx_2 \\ = & \int_{r=0}^\infty \int_{\theta = 0}^{2\pi} e^{-r^2} r dr d\theta \\ = & \int_{r=0}^\infty \int_{\theta = 0}^{2\pi} e^{-r^2} \frac{1}{2} 2 r dr d\theta \\ = & \int_{r=0}^\infty \int_{\theta = 0}^{2\pi} e^{-r^2} \frac{1}{2} dr^2 d\theta \end{align*} $$ Let, $r^2 = u$; $2r dr = du$. Then the equation becomes, $$ \begin{align*} & = \int_{u=0}^\infty \int_{\theta = 0}^{2\pi} e^{-u} \frac{1}{2} du d\theta \\ & = \frac{1}{2} \int_{\theta = 0}^{2\pi} d\theta \int_{u=0}^\infty e^{-u} du \\ & = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2\pi \left[-e^{-u}\right]_0^\infty \\ & = \pi \end{align*} $$ Now, we get, $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi}$$ Now, let's work out this integral for three dimensional case. Yes. You guessed it (hopefully), we'll take the cube of the integral this time. $$ \begin{align*} & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} dx\right)^3 \\ = & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_1^2} dx_1\right) \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_2^2} dx_2\right) \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_3^2} dx_3\right) \\ = & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2)} dx_1 dx_2 dx_3 \end{align*} $$ The transformation equations for spherical polar coordinates are, $$ \begin{align*} & x_1 = r \sin \theta \cos \phi \text{ ;} \\ & x_2 = r \sin \theta \sin \phi \text{ ;} \\ & x_3 = r \cos \theta \text{ ;} \\ & x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_3^2 = r^2 \text{ ;} \\ & dx_1 dx_2 dx_3 = r^2dr \sin \theta d\theta d\phi \end{align*} $$ The integral becomes, $$ \begin{align*} & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2+x_3^2)} dx_1 dx_2 dx_3 \\ = & \int_{\phi = 0}^{2 \pi} \int_{\theta = 0}^{\pi} \int_{r=0}^\infty e^{-r^2} r^2dr \sin \theta d\theta d\phi \\ = & \int_{\phi = 0}^{2 \pi} d\phi \int_{\theta = 0}^{\pi} \sin \theta d\theta \int_{r=0}^\infty e^{-r^2} r^2dr \\ = & 4 \pi \cdot \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{4} \\ = & \left( \sqrt{\pi} \right)^3 \end{align*} $$ Now, it's obvious that, $$ \begin{align*} & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} dx\right)^n \\ = & \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_1^2} dx_1\right) \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_2^2} dx_2\right) \cdots \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x_n^2} dx_n\right) \\ = & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \cdots \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2+ \cdots +x_n^2)} dx_1 dx_2 \cdots dx_n \\ = & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int \cdots \int e^{-r^2} dr d\phi_1 \cdots d\phi_{n-1} \\ = & \left( \sqrt{\pi} \right)^n \end{align*} $$ We need to switch from coordinates $(x_1, x_2, \cdots , x_n)$ to hyperspherical coordinates $(r,\phi_1, \cdots , \phi_n)$.
$$x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = r^2 \text{ ;} \\ dx_1 dx_2 \cdots dx_n = r^{n-1}dr d\Omega_{n-1}$$
We need to, now, understand what $d\Omega_{n-1}$ is.

Recall from the two dimensional case, $$\int d\Omega_{2-1} = \int d\Omega_1 = \int_0^{2\pi} d \theta = 2 \pi$$ This can be thought as the perimeter of a circle (2-sphere) of radius 1 unit $(2 \pi r)$. Mathematically, we can think this one dimensional perimeter as the surface area of a two dimensinal sphere.

Also recall, from the three dimensional case, $$ \begin{align*} \int d\Omega_{3-1} = \int d\Omega_2 = \int_0^{\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \sin \theta d \theta d \phi = 4 \pi \end{align*} $$ This can be thought as the surface area of a sphere (3-sphere) of radius 1 unit $(4 \pi r^2)$.

Similarly, for a n-dimensional sphere $\int d\Omega_{n-1}$ represents the surface area. $$ \begin{align*} & \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty \cdots \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x_1^2+x_2^2+ \cdots +x_n^2)} dx_1 dx_2 \cdots dx_n \\ = & \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-r^2} r^{n-1} dr \int d\Omega_{n-1} \\ \Rightarrow & \left( \sqrt{\pi} \right)^n = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-r^2} r^{n-2} \frac{1}{2} 2 r dr S_n \end{align*} $$ where, $S_n = \int d\Omega_{n-1}$ denotes the (n-1) dimensional surface area of a n-sphere.

Let, $r^2 = u$; $2r dr = du$. $$\Rightarrow \left( \pi \right)^{n/2} = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-u} \left(\sqrt{u}\right)^{n-2} \frac{1}{2} du S_n$$ $$\Rightarrow \left( \pi \right)^{n/2} = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-u} \left(u\right)^{n/2-1} \frac{1}{2} du S_n$$
Using the definition of Gamma function, $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-u} u^{n-1} du = \Gamma n$, $$\Rightarrow \left( \pi \right)^{n/2} = \frac{1}{2} \Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} \right) S_n$$ $$\Rightarrow S_n = \frac{\left( \pi \right)^{n/2}}{\frac{1}{2} \Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} \right)} $$ The integral of this surface area with respect to the $r$ coordinate, gives the volume enclosed by the surface. $$ \begin{align*} V_n & = \int S_n r^{n-1} dr \\ & = S_n \frac{r^n}{n} \\ & = \frac{\left( \pi \right)^{n/2}}{\frac{1}{2} \Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} \right)} \frac{r^n}{n} \\ & = \frac{\left( \pi \right)^{n/2}}{\frac{n}{2} \Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} \right)} r^n \\ & = \frac{\left( \pi \right)^{n/2}}{\Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} +1 \right)} r^n \end{align*} $$ where, from Gamma function, $\Gamma (n+1) = n \Gamma n$. And for a n-sphere of radius 1, the equation becomes, $$ V_n = \frac{\left( \pi \right)^{n/2}}{\Gamma \left( \frac{n}{2} +1 \right)}$$
Now, let's see the property of the Gamma function. As you see, the Gamma function increases rapidly with increase of n. from scipy.special import gamma
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n = np.linspace(-10, 10, 200)
y = gamma(n)

plt.plot(n, y)
plt.ylim(-20,20)
plt.xlim(-6, 6)
plt.xlabel("n")
plt.ylabel("$\Gamma n$")
plt.show()
Now, what about the volume? You might think that the volume increases with the dimension. So, let's see.
from scipy.special import gamma
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n = np.linspace(1, 30, 2000)
V = np.pi**(n/2)/gamma((n/2)+1)

plt.plot(n, V)
plt.xlabel("n (dimension)")
plt.ylabel("V (volume)")
plt.show()
We can see that the volume increases with the dimension upto 5 dimensions. After that it keeps going near zero with the increase of dimension.
